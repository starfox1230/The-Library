Visual Abstract
Core physics

Microbubbles are 3–5-µm purely intravascular agents whose nonlinear oscillation produces the contrast-specific signal.

Optimal setup

Use very-low-MI imaging (about <0.1), keep the target within roughly 10 cm, and place the focal zone deep to the target.

Signal tradeoff

Lower transmit frequency improves penetration and microbubble sensitivity but reduces spatial resolution.

Major pitfalls

Recognize burnoff, pseudowashout, pseudoenhancement, saturation, attenuation, shadowing, and Doppler contamination.

Corrective strategy

Use intermittent imaging, optimize gain and dose, change the acoustic window, and apply burst imaging when enhancement versus artifact is uncertain.

Introduction The introduction of ultrasound-specific contrast agents (UCAs) has led to a renaissance in US, allowing new imaging and interventional techniques that previously were not possible with US. Contrast-enhanced US (CEUS) leverages the inherent benefits of a portable, widely available, and cost-effective modality with UCAs that offer exceptional safety and other unique benefits compared with contrast agents used in CT and MRI. CEUS has been used worldwide for years, with several U.S. Food and Drug Administration–approved UCAs in the United States and reimbursement billing codes now available. With the increasing use of CEUS, it is important that radiologists recognize artifacts unique to this modality to help improve image quality, avoid misinterpretations, and develop comfort and confidence in performing the technique. Microbubble Formulation and Pharmacokinetics UCAs consist of small particles that are approximately the size of red blood cells (3–5 µm). These particles, commonly known as microbubbles, are composed of an inert, high-molecular-weight, poorly dissolvable fluorocarbon gas core encapsulated in a biocompatible lipid or protein shell. The gas is excreted by means of exhalation, while the phospholipids or proteins of the shell are processed by the reticuloendothelial system. Microbubble clearance is rapid, with a half-life of several minutes; this allows multiple injections during a single examination, providing the opportunity to image multiple organs and characterize multiple lesions. Subsequent contrast agent doses can generally be administered after two to three half-lives (or about 10 minutes). Purposeful microbubble destruction can accelerate contrast agent clearance. Although U.S. Food and Drug Administration dose limits exist, given their short half-life, higher total administered doses can be used off-label. Microbubbles are too large to pass through the vessel endothelium and are purely intravascular contrast agents. Unlike iodinated and gadolinium-based contrast agents that diffuse out of the vascular space, leading to delayed extravascular interstitial accumulation and enhancement, microbubbles function as a pure blood pool agent. This quality allows real-time angiographic imaging of vessels and perfused tissues at high temporal and spatial resolution. UCAs may also be injected into other cavities and spaces and typically will not intravasate into the blood pool. UCAs are not excreted by the kidneys, so urographic imaging may be achieved only by means of direct injection into the urinary tract. Microbubble-Ultrasound Interaction An ultrasound pulse is a mechanical wave composed of compression and rarefaction sequences at frequencies in the range of 1–20 MHz. Microbubbles contract and expand (ie, oscillate) when exposed to ultrasound at specific frequencies (resonance), usually 1–3 MHz. The degree of oscillation is dependent on the peak pressure. At pressures lower than 100 kPa, microbubbles demonstrate symmetric contraction and rarefaction, also known as linear oscillation. Between peak pressures of 100 and 500 kPa, there is a nonlinear response, meaning the degree of rarefaction is out of proportion to the degree of compression, resulting in a several-fold change in the diameter of a microbubble. This nonlinear oscillation generates strong harmonic and ultraharmonic frequencies in higher and lower multiples of the transmitted (fundamental) frequency. Most tissues demonstrate a predominantly linear response and produce return echoes at the same frequency as the transmitted pulse. Another unique physical property of microbubbles is their fragility. At peak pressures greater than 500 kPa, the degree of microbubble oscillation disrupts the shell, with immediate release of the gas core and loss of signal. Therefore, a US machine’s contrast mode relies on low-power imaging, as measured by using the mechanical index (MI), a manufacturer-specific estimate of absorbed acoustic pressure. At a low MI (<0.1), microbubbles demonstrate optimal oscillation to generate a signal while minimizing microbubble destruction. However, a higher MI (>0.5) leads to accelerated microbubble destruction. High-MI techniques may be used to purposefully clear microbubbles from the field of view; this is known as a burst, or flash, whereby a short rapid succession of high-power pulses is transmitted (Fig 3; Movies 1, 2). This technique facilitates a brief high-signal image acquisition, during which microbubbles are destroyed, with subsequent loss of the microbubble-related signal. In addition, this technique can be leveraged to evaluate enhancement patterns and the refill rate of lesions; this is especially useful for characterizing rapidly enhancing liver lesions. CEUS Image Generation CEUS image generation relies on the differences in response between microbubbles (nonlinear) and the surrounding tissues (predominantly linear). US devices with contrast agent–specific imaging software take advantage of these differences. Most US unit manufacturers use a combination of phase and amplitude modulation and harmonic filtering to null the linear responses from background tissues and produce contrast-only images. This technique, also known as real-time tissue subtraction, provides exquisite microbubble-specific signal relative to background, allowing the administration of very small contrast agent doses (less than a few milliliters for most agents) and the detection of even a single microbubble in vivo (Movie 3). The contrast-only image is often presented in an orange or sepia hue and can be displayed on its own, in dual-screen mode side by side with a gray-scale image (Figs 5, E1), or as an overlay on the gray-scale image. In contrast mode, the reference gray-scale image is reconstructed from the contrast pulse sequence and is generally of lower quality compared with a standard B-mode gray-scale image.

Technical Parameters and Image Optimization Contrast-enhanced Imaging Mode Settings Achieving an optimal balance between technical imaging parameters, scanning technique, and UCA dose is key to obtaining high-quality CEUS images. To perform high-quality imaging and minimize artifacts, it is important to understand and optimize the technical parameters that influence the contrast mode images. Although the specific names and attributes of the adjustable parameters may vary among manufacturers, contemporary contrast settings are generally preoptimized and require minimal, if any, adjustment. User-adjustable settings may include output power, image gain, frame rate, focal zone, transducer frequency, depth, and sector width. Table 1: Scanning Parameters and Techniques Affecting CEUS Study Quality Output Power.—The amplitude of the transmitted pulse, referred to as output power and related to the MI, must be low enough during contrast-enhanced imaging to minimize bubble destruction while maintaining an adequate signal-to-noise ratio. US machine vendors provide standard output power settings that are optimized for most scenarios, which generally do not require adjusting. In select situations (deep lesions, obese patients, hepatic steatosis), it may be appropriate to increase the output power to achieve additional penetration. Although increasing the output power will result in increased backscatter signal from microbubbles, it may also increase image noise and accentuate some artifacts such as pseudoenhancement. (See the “Bubble Destruction” section.) Increasing output power also accelerates microbubble destruction, decreases the contrast agent half-life, and may cause pseudowashout. If the output power is too low, the contrast mode image will be too dark. Image Gain.—Image gain, a postprocessing parameter, reflects the overall amplification of returning echoes and affects image brightness. When the contrast-enhanced image gain is correctly adjusted, the organ or tissue of interest is black or nearly black, leaving only a few highly echogenic interfaces visible. When the image gain is set too high, the precontrast image shows diffuse bright pixels, indicating noise. When the gain is set too low, the contrast-enhanced image may be too dark for interpretation. Therefore, it is recommended that highly echogenic interfaces, such as fascial planes, organ capsules, vessel walls, bowel gas, and calcifications, remain visible. As it is difficult to eliminate all echoes from the contrast-only image before contrast agent administration, careful comparison of the precontrast versus postcontrast images may be necessary to ensure that a signal seen on the postinjection postcontrast image is from microbubbles and not due to artifact. The image gain should be adjusted before UCA administration. Adjusting the gain after UCA administration is generally not recommended. Although increasing the gain can result in increased brightness from the microbubbles, it also amplifies noise, and differentiating enhancing from nonenhancing structures may be difficult. If quantitative perfusion measurements are needed, the contrast presets should not be adjusted, as this may alter the input signal and lead to errors in time-intensity curve analysis. Similar to overall gain adjustments, time-gain-compensation adjustments should be made before the contrast agent administration to achieve image uniformity from the near field to the far field. Fine adjustments can be made to compensate for excessive attenuation by amplifying the signal from the far field, although these adjustments should be made with caution, given the risk of amplifying noise and pseudoenhancement. Frame Rate.—The frame rate is the number of images displayed per unit of time, expressed in hertz. The frame rate generally is automatically set to the maximal value that is allowable, depending on the sector width, image depth, and other settings. Some systems allow a user to decrease the frame rate (sometimes referred to as the pulse repetition frequency). Although high frame rates allow higher temporal resolution, they may also lead to accelerated microbubble destruction. In contrast mode, frame rates may be reduced owing to the multipulse techniques used for contrast-enhanced imaging. Reducing the frame rate further may minimize microbubble destruction, and this may be helpful in evaluating lesions with slow washout, such as some hepatocellular carcinomas. Conversely, rapidly enhancing lesions may need a higher frame rate for adequate assessment of high-flow structures or lesions. Focal Zone.—Ultrasound energy deposition and, thus, microbubble disruption, is not uniform throughout the image. Much of the ultrasound beam energy is concentrated at the focal zone. To minimize microbubble destruction, the focal zone should generally be positioned well beyond (deep to) the target. Many users place the focal zone at the lowest depth in the field of view. When assessing focal liver lesions, it may be appropriate to place the focal zone just below the lesion of interest to balance spatial resolution and microbubble destruction. Transducer Frequency.—Optimal microbubble resonance is achieved at lower frequencies (1–3 MHz). Although imaging at lower frequencies improves penetration, it negatively impacts spatial resolution. Higher-frequency curvilinear transducers and high-frequency linear transducers may be used for CEUS to improve spatial resolution; however, the signal from microbubbles may not be as strong, resulting in an overall darker contrast-enhanced image. Also, at higher frequencies, a smaller distribution of microbubble sizes will be stimulated. For these reasons, a higher contrast agent dose—up to 1.5 to two times the dose of that for the liver or kidney—may be needed. Preparing for Success Before performing CEUS, it is important to optimize the patient positioning and transducer orientation to maximize visualization of the region of interest during the examination. The following are a few tips to achieve these goals. Ergonomics.—Before the UCA is injected, both the sonographer and the patient should be in a comfortable position that can be maintained for the duration of the examination. As imaging may last for several minutes and several injections may be attempted, the patient needs to be in a comfortable position to minimize movement, which may alter the acoustic window. Similarly, as a specific orientation will be maintained for a prolonged period, the sonographer should ensure that their body, arm, and hand are supported and relaxed to minimize cramping and body strain. Depth and Patient Positioning.—Depth is generally a limiting factor in B-mode imaging, CEUS in particular. Loss of signal in the far field is intrinsic to US and occurs owing to attenuation of the ultrasound pulse. Given the low–output-power (ie, low-MI) mode required for contrast-enhanced imaging, lesions deeper than 10 cm can be challenging to image. If a lesion is not well seen on gray-scale images, visualizing it in contrast mode will be difficult. To best visualize the contrast signal response, it is important to decrease the distance between the area of interest and the transducer, often by adjusting the patient’s position or changing the transducer orientation to demonstrate the target within a depth of 10 cm. Several patient positions and acoustic windows from various directions (lateral, posterior, or intercostal) should be attempted to maximize visualization while minimizing the target-to-transducer distance. Decreasing the transducer transmit frequency may improve visualization of deeper structures, but it negatively impacts spatial resolution. If additional signal from deeper structures is needed or in livers that are difficult to image (such as in cirrhosis or steatosis), the output power can be increased with caution. Acoustic Window.— An acoustic window that allows visualization of the target with the least amount of depth and minimal shadowing from gas or ribs is ideal. When possible, the target should be imaged in a plane (typically sagittal) that keeps the area of interest in the field of view during quiet respiration and enables visualization of the adjacent normal parenchyma for comparison. Ideally, one should avoid using the liver or spleen as an acoustic window (eg, when evaluating a renal lesion), as the overlying enhancing parenchyma may lead to accentuated attenuation and some forms of pseudoenhancement. (See the “Pseudoenhancement” and “Attenuation and Shadowing” sections.) Contrast Agent Dose.—Contemporary ultrasound devices and software generally offer higher sensitivity to UCAs than older-generation systems. Therefore, the doses of intravenously injected contrast agents are generally lower than the doses of contrast media listed in U.S. Food and Drug Administraton documents and on package inserts. The contrast agent dose may vary, depending on the organ or tissue of interest, with vascular applications and organs with large relative blood volume (such as the liver and kidneys) requiring smaller doses than targets with lower relative blood volume (eg, testes). As microbubbles are highly reflective, excessive concentrations may lead to undesirable attenuation and even acoustic shadowing, particularly in intracavitary applications. Dose-related effects on image quality are detailed in the “Artifacts from Microbubbles” section. For subsequent injections, waiting for complete clearance of previously injected microbubbles is recommended, particularly if perfusion analysis is desired. To shorten the contrast agent half-life and the wait time between injections, standard B-mode imaging, color Doppler US, or the scanner’s burst, or flash, pulse sequence can be used to rapidly destroy bubbles. It is important to note that microbubble destruction results in high mechanical energy deposition, so users should follow the ALARA (as low as reasonably achievable) principles when imaging potentially sensitive tissue.

Image Artifacts An imaging artifact is an image finding or feature that does not accurately represent the object being imaged. Artifacts are routinely encountered on medical images of any modality. US image artifacts may be due to improper technique or an unexpected interaction within the object being imaged or may be intrinsic to the modality (eg, processing error). Many US artifacts are unwanted and can obscure anatomy, degrade image quality, or simulate disease, leading to confusion and errors in image interpretation. However, some US artifacts are useful in making certain diagnoses or can provide information related to the composition of the structure or tissue being imaged (eg, increased through- transmission or posterior acoustic enhancement of cysts, acoustic shadowing of stones). CEUS artifacts may be related to common B-mode artifacts, specific to contrast-enhanced imaging techniques, or related to the microbubbles themselves. In the remainder of this article, we review many of these artifacts, with an emphasis on their imaging appearances and the strategies to minimize their impact. Standard B-mode Artifacts on CEUS Images For B-mode image formation, ultrasound equipment relies on specific physical assumptions to assign the location and intensity of each echo. In clinical scanning, when assumptions regarding ultrasound beam characteristics, echo path, speed of sound, and attenuation are violated, various artifacts may become apparent on the gray-scale image. The common artifacts seen with B-mode US, including posterior acoustic shadowing and enhancement; signal attenuation, refraction, and reflection; and reverberation, are often apparent and may be accentuated at CEUS. Table 2: Common US Artifacts on CEUS Images Posterior Acoustic Shadowing and Enhancement.—Posterior acoustic shadowing results from highly attenuating and/or reflective structures such as stones, calcified atherosclerotic plaque, and air-filled bowel, resulting in absent backscatter signal from deeper structures. Although this is commonly seen on the gray-scale image, shadowing may appear accentuated at CEUS as the output power is decreased, and spatial compounding is deactivated in the contrast mode. Shadowing may obscure the target area of interest, in which case changes in acoustic window or altering the patient position may be needed. Posterior acoustic enhancement (ie, increased through- transmission) occurs when weakly attenuating structures with minimal contrast enhancement (classically cysts) show increased amplitude of deeper echoes. Acoustic enhancement may also be seen on CEUS images; however, corrective action is typically not needed. Signal Attenuation.—Ultrasound beam attenuation on contrast-enhanced images appears as progressive loss of microbubble signal in the far field. The contrast mode is a low-output-power (low-MI) mode, which impacts the acoustic energy of returning echoes. Although the output power can be increased, this comes at the expense of increased microbubble destruction, particularly in the near field. Alternatively, a lower transmit frequency transducer may improve the signal from the far field. As previously mentioned, repositioning the patient to decrease the distance between the transducer and the region of interest may diminish the impact of attenuation. Refraction and Reflection.—Refraction and reflection artifacts manifest owing to the assumption that ultrasound pulses travel in a straight line to and from a target. Refraction artifacts are typically seen as bands of low signal extending vertically along the axial scanning direction, with the apex at the margin of a curved interface separating two tissues (“edge shadow”). If the target area of interest is obscured by refractive shadowing, changes in the acoustic window or patient repositioning may change the angle of incidence enough to minimize or eliminate this artifact. Reflection artifact also occurs owing to the error in assuming that an ultrasound pulse propagates in a single straight path. A classic mirror image artifact is caused by a highly reflective curved surface, such as the diaphragm, that acts as a mirror. This artifact is generally not a diagnostic dilemma, as it is typically sufficient to recognize and disregard the duplicated structure, often mapped in the chest cavity. Reverberation.—Reverberation results in image clutter in the near field, or sequential equally spaced bright lines deep to a pair of echogenic interfaces. This artifact originates from the assumption that there is only a single reflection per ultrasound pulse. In practice, there are many internal reflections, as returning echoes may encounter additional interfaces that further split the acoustic energy into transmitted and re-reflected components. If an ultrasound pulse encounters two relatively closely spaced echogenic interfaces, multiple internal reflections will result in echoes returning later and thus being mapped in the image deeper than the physical location of the structure. For multiple closely apposed interfaces, such as those in the anterior abdominal wall, the consolidation of echoes may result in diffuse image clutter in the near field. Other commonly encountered B-mode artifacts, such as those caused by side lobes and elevation plane volume averaging, can also be seen at CEUS, but these are not described in this article.

Contrast Mode–specific Artifacts Several artifacts are related to the specific techniques used for CEUS. Table 3: CEUS-specific Artifacts Suppression and Nonlinear Artifacts The tissue subtraction (suppression of signal from background tissues) achieved by using the contrast mode is highly effective for homogeneous tissues. As discussed earlier, some structures, interfaces, and tissues may remain echogenic on the contrast-only image obtained before contrast agent administration, despite appropriate image gain. This is most apparent at highly echogenic interfaces such as abdominal wall fascial planes, organ capsules, calcifications, bowel lumens, and vessel walls. These artifacts can usually be identified before the injection and have a correlate structure on the corresponding gray-scale image. This is of particular importance immediately following percutaneous ablation, when large bubbles within the ablation bed may remain apparent on the contrast-only image. Although most tissues respond linearly to an ultrasound pulse and are easily nulled on the contrast-only image, a smaller component of the response may be nonlinear, resulting in incomplete suppression. Phase aberrations that occur in some tissues will result in less effective subtraction and are most commonly seen in the subcutaneous adipose tissue and steatotic liver. Ultrasound pulses propagating through a large number of microbubbles result in a nonlinear artifactual signal from deeper structures, leading to pseudoenhancement. (See the “Pseudoenhancement” section.) If differentiating truly enhancing tissues from pseudoenhancement remains a diagnostic dilemma, the burst, or flash, feature can be used to purposely destroy the microbubble-related signal within the field of view. For truly enhancing tissues, the signal will be immediately lost and reperfusion may be seen, whereas artifactual signal will have a similar appearance before and after microbubble destruction. Bubble Destruction Unexpected loss of the contrast signal may be the result of unintentional microbubble destruction. Even with a microbubble-specific low-MI imaging mode, a small proportion of microbubbles may be disrupted with each incident pulse, and this destruction may not be uniform throughout the target or tissue. Continuous imaging, higher frame rates, and higher output power will exacerbate the artifacts discussed in the sections that follow. To preserve microbubbles (ie, increase the contrast agent half-life) and minimize some of these artifacts, brief intervals of viewing and imaging (every 30–60 seconds after the arterial phase) can be interleaved with periods of nonimaging when the image is “frozen”; this technique is called intermittent imaging. Returning to the standard B-mode or initiating color Doppler US will rapidly destroy microbubbles and should be avoided if contrast preservation is desired. Near-Field Signal Loss.—Near-field signal loss manifests as a progressive asymmetric decrease in the microbubble-related signal within the tissues closest to the transducer, leading to a dark horizontal band in the near field. Acoustic energy absorption is not uniform within the field of view and is greatest in the superficial tissues and at the focal zone. This asymmetric energy deposition, often called bubble “burnoff,” results in greater microbubble destruction in the near field and is accentuated with a higher MI, with higher scanning frequencies, and at higher frame rates. Corrective measures include use of lower frame rates, intermittent imaging, and frequent changes in transducer orientation. Imaging Plane Signal Loss.—In-plane signal loss is seen as diffuse signal loss in large areas of imaged tissue and is often recognized when sweeping through a region previously imaged or changing the transducer orientation to an orthogonal plane in which a band of low signal will be evident (Fig 19, Movie 2). This artifact results from accelerated microbubble destruction in the imaging plane relative to adjacent out-of-plane tissues and is accentuated when the transducer remains in a single location for a prolonged period, leading to a population of microbubbles receiving continuous insonation. This phenomenon may be mitigated by ensuring low-MI imaging, decreasing the frame rate, leveraging intermittent imaging, and sweeping through an area instead of remaining in one fixed location. Pseudowashout.—The asymmetric loss of contrast signal may also result from nonuniform destruction of microbubbles between tissues—that is, pseudowashout—as opposed to the true washout seen in lesions with distinctly different perfusion characteristics. Pseudowashout can be seen in slow-flow lesions such as hemangiomas. If microbubbles are replenished at a slower rate or destroyed at a higher rate, this may lead to alterations in the appearance of the washout. This phenomenon is of particular importance in liver imaging, when washout is one characteristic that is critical for lesion characterization. Pseudowashout may then lead to diagnostic dilemmas or even misdiagnoses. Again, decreasing the frame rate, leveraging intermittent imaging, making sure that the focal zone is beyond the area of interest, and sweeping through a lesion instead of prolonged continuous stationary imaging may help eliminate this artifact. Artifacts from Microbubbles Finally, some artifacts are related directly to the microbubbles themselves. Table 4: Artifacts from Microbubbles Pseudoenhancement.—Similar to phase aberrations accumulated by propagating ultrasound pulses traveling through fatty tissues, phase aberrations in CEUS result from a large population of microbubbles superficial to a target. This may manifest as hypoenhancing or nonenhancing echogenic structures or tissues showing an artifactual increase in signal after contrast agent administration (pseudoenhancement). This form of pseudoenhancement is not apparent before the injection of microbubbles, differentiating it from incompletely suppressed echogenic interfaces that appear the same before and following contrast agent administration. This artifact is most commonly seen when imaging through the liver or spleen and worsens with increasing bubble concentration and increasing image gain or output power. To minimize this artifact, decrease the skin-to-target distance and attempt to use an acoustic window that minimizes or eliminates intervening, highly enhancing tissue between the transducer and target. Pseudoenhancement is particularly troublesome following ablation (such as radiofrequency or microwave ablation), when highly echogenic material may remain within the ablation bed. Nonlinear artifact may manifest as a nonmarginal (central) signal within the ablation zone that increases after contrast agent administration and that can be mistaken for enhancement. However, true enhancement from residual or recurrent viable disease typically appears at the periphery during the arterial phase, instead of centrally, and often later in the portal venous phase, as with pseudoenhancement. Pseudoenhancement may be minimized by choosing the appropriate power and gain settings before the contrast material injection and reducing the dose of the injected agent. Finally, if the gain or output power is inappropriately increased in an attempt to improve the signal from the far field or increase the signal from microbubbles in the late phase after the contrast agent injection, noise and any incompletely suppressed signal from phase aberrations may lead to pseudoenhancement. Signal Saturation.—Signal saturation occurs when the signal intensity of echoes overpowers the scanner’s maximal allowable backscatter signal and is higher than is allowed on the display. After contrast agent administration, some highly enhancing structures may appear nearly white on the contrast-only image, mapped to color values at the top of the scale. The causes of this phenomenon include high output power or a high dose of UCA. Signal saturation occurs more commonly in renal transplantation or angiographic studies owing to the relatively high concentration of microbubbles or when imaging highly vascular lesions. A uniformly intense signal throughout a perfused organ or structure can mask underlying subtle changes of early enhancement or washout and confound the interpretation of contrast kinetics at time-intensity curve analysis. The time-intensity curve misinterpretation occurs when the highest possible pixel value, displayed as a plateau, is reached, and, thus, the true peak enhancement may not be detectable. Strategies to mitigate this artifact include decreasing the contrast agent dose and decreasing the output power, although the latter approach should be used with caution when attempting perfusion quantification. Attenuation and Shadowing.— The attenuation and shadowing seen on gray-scale images also appear on contrast-enhanced images. Microbubbles also absorb and reflect acoustic energy, and a high concentration of microbubbles in the near field can result in accentuated attenuation, leading to poor visualization of deeper structures. This is commonly encountered in highly perfused organs, such as the liver, resulting in a markedly attenuated signal from the right kidney. Waiting for clearance of microbubbles from the near field or decreasing the contrast material dose at a subsequent injection may be helpful. An alternative acoustic window that minimizes intervening structures between the transducer and target can also be attempted. The reflection and absorption effect of a high concentration of microbubbles can be so great as to lead to posterior acoustic shadowing. This is most commonly encountered with intracavitary contrast agent injections such as those in contrast-enhanced vesicourosonography or antegrade pyelography. Care must be taken to appropriately dilute the UCA (typically with normal saline) to prevent excessive attenuation and shadowing in these applications. Color Doppler Artifacts.—The presence of microbubbles in the circulation can affect the color Doppler signal from vessels. The marked increase in backscatter signal from the microbubbles significantly increases the detection of Doppler shifts, resulting in an increase in color Doppler signal to a degree such that color is mapped beyond the physical vessel wall (“blooming” artifact), wherein the color appears to “bleed” out from the vessel lumen. Correction can be achieved by increasing the Doppler scale and decreasing the Doppler gain. As the color Doppler mode is a relatively high-MI mode, rapid microbubble destruction will occur when this mode is activated. This rapid destruction of microbubbles within the parenchyma of an organ may be misinterpreted as the Doppler shifts, and a mosaic of color combinations is seen diffusely throughout the parenchyma. This finding generally resolves within a few seconds of scanning. Pulse-Wave Doppler Artifacts.—Artifact from intravascular gas has a characteristic appearance on pulse-wave Doppler US images. The classic example is that of gas bubbles in the portal vein that result in high-amplitude or high-intensity spikes superimposed on the normal portal vein spectral Doppler waveform tracing. In a similar manner, if spectral Doppler imaging is attempted shortly after UCA injection, these high-intensity spikes may be superimposed on spectral waveforms, simulating portal venous gas. In addition, an increase in the overall Doppler signal may overcome the filters designed to reduce noise. These phenomena may lead to an error in estimating the correct peak systolic velocity, especially if automatic calculation software is used. It is recommended that any needed Doppler US examination be performed before UCA administration. Otherwise, the sonographer will need to wait a sufficient amount of time for complete microbubble clearance (at least three half-lives, or 10–15 minutes). Persistent Heterogeneous Liver Enhancement.—Adverse events following UCA administration are exceedingly rare. One rare phenomenon, known as persistent heterogeneous liver enhancement (PHLE), manifests as markedly heterogeneous echogenic liver parenchyma that appears cloudy or wool-like on gray-scale images, and as patchy echogenicity on contrast-enhanced images. This finding may appear shortly after UCA administration and can last for several hours, slowly resolving over the subsequent 1–2 days. PHLE has been reported more commonly in young thin patients who received multiple or large contrast agent doses, and there appears to be a correlation with enteritis and colitis. The cause of PHLE is unknown, although hypotheses include fusion of multiple microbubbles and insufflation and expansion of circulating microbubbles due to bowel gas from an inflamed bowel, resulting in embolization of the macrobubbles into the liver. These hypotheses are supported by the finding that these large bubbles are generally seen only in the portal circulation, are seen on both contrast-only and gray-scale images, and are not destroyed by high-MI imaging techniques. The imaging appearance may raise concern, given the features that overlap with those of portal venous gas in necrotizing enterocolitis. However, several retrospective reviews have shown no adverse outcomes, and patients universally remain asymptomatic.

Conclusion All imaging modalities have artifacts that may negatively impact examination quality and diagnostic confidence. Understanding the technical principles behind these artifacts allows users to modify their scanning technique to minimize many of these artifacts. For interpreters of CEUS studies, identifying and possibly correcting these artifacts facilitate higher image quality, improved diagnostic accuracy, and better patient care.
