ARTICLE PROSE

Incidental findings are abnormalities detected on imaging obtained for unrelated indications. Although a minority represent clinically important disease, most are benign and can trigger unnecessary follow-up, procedures, patient anxiety, cost, and cascades of care.

The article updates management of abdominal and pelvic incidental findings in light of evidence published after the ACR white papers. It emphasizes risk stratification by patient context, imaging morphology, lesion size, and whether the finding is covered by existing guidance.

Hepatic findings include hyperenhancing liver lesions at CT and echogenic liver lesions at US. Small homogeneous flash-filling liver lesions in low-risk patients generally do not need follow-up, while larger homogeneous or heterogeneously enhancing lesions and suspicious findings in high-risk patients warrant MRI or other workup.

Biliary updates include asymptomatic biliary ductal dilatation, focal fundal gallbladder wall thickening, and gallbladder polyps. Ductal dilatation without an obstructing cause and with normal liver function tests often does not require follow-up, while suspicious gallbladder wall features or higher-risk polyps require more directed evaluation.

Renal and adrenal sections address indeterminate homogeneous renal masses at CT, hyperechoic renal lesions at US, T1-hyperintense renal lesions at MRI, and adrenal nodules. Homogeneous renal masses measuring 21-30 HU at portal venous phase CT are highly likely benign, while higher attenuation, heterogeneity, enhancement, or risk factors should prompt further characterization.

Splenic, bowel, mesenteric, and male reproductive findings are managed by risk context. Isolated benign-appearing splenic lesions in low-risk patients usually do not need follow-up, short uncomplicated small bowel intussusceptions are often transient, misty mesentery without lymphadenopathy is usually benign, and testicular microlithiasis in low-risk patients does not warrant imaging follow-up.

Focal Fundal Gallbladder Wall Thickening Focal gallbladder wall thickening can occur anywhere along the wall but is most common at the fundus. The most common benign cause is adenomyomatosis, although other causes include benign polyps and gallbladder malignancy. The white paper on incidental biliary lesions does not make specific recommendations on focal wall thickening based on CT findings, and instead recommends follow-up actions based on size, clinical factors, and correlation with US to assess for features of adenomyomatosis (10). A study by Corwin et al (34) identified morphologic features of incidentally detected fundal wall thickening at CECT that are useful in distinguishing benign adenomyomatosis from malignancy. Focal gallbladder wall thickening with definite internal cystic spaces is compatible with adenomyomatosis (Fig 9) (34,35). Fundal thickening with homogeneous intramural low attenuation (which represents cystic spaces below the resolution of CT), often with a pinched or rounded morphology (Fig 10), is almost certainly benign adenomyomatosis. In contrast, one of six cases with full-thickness homogeneous wall enhancement (Fig 11) and three of 10 cases with heterogeneous fundal wall enhancement proved to be malignant (34). 23. Figure 23. Misty mesentery in a 41-year-old man with known lymphoma. Misty mesentery with associated lymphadenopathy requires further evaluation with follow-up imaging, PET/CT, or biopsy, depending on the degree of lymphadenopathy.
