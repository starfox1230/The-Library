Introduction The Lung CT Screening Reporting and Data System (LungRADS) was created by the American College of Radiology to standardize reporting and management recommendations for lung cancer screening and facilitate evaluation of quality and patient outcomes. Since 2014, two updates have been released: Lung-RADS version 1.1 in 2019 and Lung-RADS version 2022 in November 2022. Each iteration aims to incorporate knowledge gained from the latest lung nodule, lung cancer, and lung cancer screening research; clarify or include topics not addressed in previous versions; and reduce overdiagnosis. Using evidence-based medicine, recommendations include expert consensus where gaps in scientific knowledge exist. In this update, we review some key changes made in Lung-RADS 2022 and summarize these changes in Tables 1 and 2.

New Additions Atypical Pulmonary Cysts Lung cancers associated with cystic spaces are increasingly recognized, with approximately 0.5%–9.3% having a cystic component at initial imaging. These lesions can be underrecognized, which can result in delayed diagnosis. LungRADS 2022 adds new criteria for classification and manage - ment of atypical pulmonary cysts, which fall under categories 3 through 4B. Cyst features considered suspicious (atypical) include thick or asymmetric walls, an associated nodule, internal septations, or growth (Figs 1, 2). Cavitary Nodules Lung-RADS 2022 recommends managing cavitary nodules as solid nodules based on total mean diameter. Distinguishing a cavitary nodule from an atypical lung cyst should be based on the dominant feature. If the solid component is dominant, it is managed as a solid nodule.

If the cystic component is dominant, it is managed as an atypical lung cyst. If infection is suspected, category 0 is recommended, with follow-up low-dose chest CT in 1–3 months. Updates and Clarifications Juxtapleural Nodules Intrapulmonary lymph nodes may occur on any pleural sur - face, including fissures and the costal, mediastinal, and diaphragmatic pleura. Earlier literature reported that perifissural nodules are detected on 20%–28% of lung cancer screening examinations and are believed to represent benign lymph nodes. Also, when using a strict definition based on size and shape, a large series showed that none are malignant at follow-up. Thus, Lung-RADS 1.1 added perifissural nodules to category 2, using the following definition from de Hoop et al in the largest series published from the NELSON (Nederlands–Leuvens Longkanker Screenings Onderzoek) trial: solid nodules with smooth margins; an oval, lentiform, or triangular shape; and a maximum diameter of less than 10 mm.

Lung-RADS 2022 introduced the term juxtapleural nodules to also include nodules that meet the same definition of perifissural nodules located on the costal, mediastinal, and diaphragmatic pleura. This was based on new data showing that the criteria for perifissural nodules can be safely applied to juxtapleural nodules, whether they are found at baseline screening or develop on future screening examinations. Airway Nodules Endobronchial nodules are now referred to as airway nodules, with updated classification and management based on suspicion for malignancy. Nodule Growth An updated definition of nodule growth clarifies that the increase in size should be the mean nodule diameter (previously November 2023 Martin et al any dimension) and specifies that the time interval to assess growth is 12 months (previously not stated).

Furthermore, a new note clarifies that a nodule should be reclassified when it crosses a size threshold between categories and should be managed accordingly, regardless of whether the definition of growth is met or not. Slow Growing Nodules Slow growing nodules are addressed in Lung-RADS 2022 notes 7 and 8 based on attenuation. Slow growing groundglass nodules are assigned to category 2 if they do not grow greater than 1.5 mm over 12 months, even if growth occurs over serial examinations. If other signs of growth develop, such as a new solid component, they should be managed as part-solid nodules. Slow growing solid and part-solid nodules are managed differently than ground-glass nodules. If these nodules grow less than 1.5 mm over 12 months but growth greater than 1.5 mm occurs over multiple screening examinations, they may be upgraded to category 4B, with a recommendation for diagnostic evaluation.

Stepped Management Approach The stepped management of Lung-RADS 2022 addresses the timing of follow-up examinations and simplifies categorization of nodules at follow-up. The timing of follow-up lowdose chest CT should be based on the date of the current ex - amination and no longer on the baseline low-dose chest CT examination that triggered the follow-up examination. Infectious or Inflammatory Findings Lung-RADS 1.1 introduced the option that potentially infectious and inflammatory nodules could be categorized as 4B or 4X with the option to follow-up with low-dose chest CT in 1 month to ensure resolution. Lung-RADS 2022 describes the broad imaging features suggesting infection or inflammation and reassigns these findings to categories 0 and 2. The follow-up options include low-dose chest CT in 1–3 months or 1 year, respectively. Category 0 findings are considered indeterminate for infection or inflammation. Examples include multiple (more than six) new nodules, new solid nodule(s) greater than or equal to 8 mm appearing in a short interval, and segmental or lobar consolidation.

Category 2 findings are considered benign and include new tree-in-bud nodules or a new ground-glass nodule less than 3 cm. Segmental and/or multiple tubular endobronchial abnormalities with no central obstructing lesion could be assigned to categories 0 or 2 at the discretion of the radiologist. Conclusion Standardization and data collection are critical elements of any screening program. By addressing unclear scenarios, the LungRADS updates continue to promote these elements and the goal of reducing lung cancer mortality with lung cancer screening.
