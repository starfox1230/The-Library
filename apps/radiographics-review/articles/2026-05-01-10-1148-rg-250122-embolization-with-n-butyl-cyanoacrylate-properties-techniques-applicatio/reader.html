Visual Abstract
Core concept

N-butyl cyanoacrylate (NBCA) glue embolization is a minimally invasive technique widely used in interventional radiology to achieve vascular occlusion.

How it works

N-butyl cyanoacrylate (NBCA) glue embolization is a liquid embolic agent that rapidly polymerizes on contact with ionic substances such as blood or saline, allowing immediate solidification and formation of a durable embolus.

Clinical use

N-butyl cyanoacrylate (NBCA) glue embolization is especially effective in controlling arterial bleeding from small-caliber or tortuous vessels that cannot be accessed by using microcatheters or in cases requiring urgent hemostasis due to severe hemorrhage.

Technical pearl

Under fluoroscopic guidance, the mixture is injected through a microcatheter by using pressure- or flow-dependent techniques.

Main risk

Despite the effectiveness of NBCA glue embolization, the risk of nontarget embolization remains a significant concern, potentially resulting in tissue necrosis or ischemia.

Introduction N-butyl cyanoacrylate (NBCA) has emerged as a pivotal embolic agent in interventional radiology, facilitating a versatile and highly effective method for achieving vascular occlusion. Initially developed as a surgical adhesive, NBCA rapidly polymerizes on contact with ionic fluids such as blood, forming a solid and durable cast within seconds. This property underpins its utility in a range of embolization procedures that require prompt and permanent vessel closure. NBCA is indicated in a broad spectrum of clinical applications, including embolization for gastrointestinal hemorrhage, portal vein embolization, and lymphatic embolization. Despite its proven efficacy, the use of NBCA is associated with notable technical challenges and potential risks. The rapid polymerization of this agent demands a high level of operator expertise and careful handling to avoid adverse events such as nontarget embolization, tissue ischemia, and catheter entrapment. This article provides a comprehensive overview of NBCA embolization, with an emphasis on its chemical characteristics, technical considerations, clinical indications, and safety precautions.

Properties Development History Cyanoacrylate tissue adhesives were first developed in the 1950s, primarily for use in surgical applications. In the 1970s, isobutyl cyanoacrylate emerged as an intra-arterial embolic agent, marking the initial adoption of cyanoacrylates for endovascular therapy. However, the use of these agents was discontinued in the late 1980s owing to reports of sarcoma development in animal studies. Since then, NBCA has become the primary cyanoacrylate compound used in interventional radiology. Commercially available formulations such as Histoacryl (B. Braun) and Trufill (Johnson & Johnson MedTech) are now widely used in the field of interventional radiology. Glubran2 (GEM) is a mixture of NBCA and metacryloxysulpholane with slower polymerization. While Trufill is approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration for treatment of brain arteriovenous malformations (AVMs) and used off-label for other endovascular embolization procedures, Glubran2 holds Conformité Européenne (CE) certification for endovascular embolization, and Histoacryl is used off-label for interventional procedures. Chemical Properties The monomeric form of cyanoacrylates is an ethylene molecule (C2H4) with a cyano group (CN) and an ester group (COO) attached to the hydrocarbons. The hydrocarbons determine the name of the cyanoacrylate glue (eg, methyl cyanoacrylate, NBCA, or octyl cyanoacrylate), and a longer hydrocarbon results in a slower polymerization rate, less heat produced during polymerization, and decreased histotoxicity. The polymerization of NBCA is initiated on exposure to ionic substances, such as blood components, triggering the rapid bonding of ethylene monomers. This reaction results in the formation of a solid polymer that provides stable and permanent occlusion. NBCA is inherently hydrophobic; this property limits its dispersion in the bloodstream and facilitates precise localization at the target site. Furthermore, its strong adhesive characteristics enable firm attachment to the vessel wall, reducing the risk of migration and ensuring long-term occlusion efficacy. Biologic Effects The polymerization of NBCA is an exothermic reaction that can generate temperatures of up to 80°C (176°F), potentially resulting in pain during the procedure. The thermal effect and the ischemia caused by arterial embolization contribute to an acute inflammatory response within the vessel wall and surrounding tissues. This acute phase typically progresses to a chronic granulomatous reaction characterized by the accumulation of foreign-body giant cells and the development of fibrosis. Glubran2 forms a more flexible and stable polymer, with a lower exothermic reaction (45°C, 113°F), leading to reduced inflammation and tissue toxicity compared with those associated with Histoacryl or Trufill. NBCA is regarded as a permanent embolic agent due to its durable polymerized form. However, it is not entirely inert over time; slow resorption of the material may occur in certain cases, and recanalization of the embolized vessel has been reported occasionally. Role of Lipiodol Lipiodol (Guerbet), an iodized oil–based contrast agent, has an essential role in NBCA embolization procedures. When mixed with NBCA, it has several critical functions. First, it acts as a diluent, decreasing the viscosity of NBCA and thereby facilitating smoother delivery through the microcatheter. Second, the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio directly influences the speed of polymerization: Lower concentrations of NBCA (ie, higher proportions of Lipiodol) delay polymerization, allowing the embolic mixture to travel a greater distance; this is particularly advantageous when targeting distal or tortuous vessels. Third, due to its inherent radiopacity, Lipiodol enables real-time fluoroscopic visualization of the embolic mixture during delivery. This enables precise monitoring of its distribution, ensuring accurate deployment and reducing the risk of nontarget embolization.

Embolization Procedure Glue embolization is a sophisticated procedure that requires meticulous planning and precise execution to achieve effective vascular occlusion while minimizing complications. Pressure- or Flow-dependent Injection and Flow-controlled Injection The injection techniques used in NBCA embolization can be broadly categorized into pressure-dependent and flow-dependent methods on the basis of the size of the target vessel relative to the size of the microcatheter (Movie 1). When the size of the target vessel is similar to or smaller than the diameter of the microcatheter, the catheter can be advanced until it becomes wedged within the vessel. In this scenario, the distribution of NBCA is primarily determined by the injection pressure, constituting a pressure-dependent injection. This technique is commonly used in arterial bleeding cases, in which controlled delivery is critical. Conversely, when the target vessel is larger than the microcatheter diameter, the distribution of the embolic agent is governed predominantly by blood flow dynamics, resulting in a flow-dependent injection. This approach is frequently used in venous embolization procedures, in which the embolic material is carried along by the bloodstream to the target site (Figs 2B, 3A). Generally, pressure-dependent injection offers more predictability and control, allowing the operator to achieve precise embolization. In contrast, flow-dependent injection is inherently less predictable and offers more limited control of the glue distribution. When it is difficult for the microcatheter to reach the target lesion and NBCA must be delivered over a long distance, or when injecting glue into a large vessel with high flow dynamics, a balloon catheter can be used to control the glue distribution. This flow-controlled injection involves using a balloon catheter to temporarily arrest blood flow or reduce it to a low-flow state, allowing more controlled NBCA delivery and minimizing fragmentation of the glue cast. NBCA-to-Lipiodol Ratio The selection of the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio is guided by operator technique and the vascular anatomy. The distribution of the glue is influenced by multiple interrelated factors, including the injection technique (pressure vs flow dependent), injection speed, blood flow velocity, vascular geometry, and viscosity determined by using the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio. With pressure-dependent injections, the glue distribution is governed predominantly by the injection speed, rendering the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio relatively less critical. In such cases, typical NBCA-to-Lipiodol dilution ratios range from 1:2 to 1:3, providing manageable viscosity while ensuring timely polymerization. In contrast, flow-dependent injections are significantly more sensitive to the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio. With these injections, the glue distribution is shaped by a combination of injection speed, hemodynamic conditions, and glue viscosity, making operator experience essential for effective and safe delivery. In high-flow vessels, a higher NBCA concentration (eg, 1:1 or 1:2) combined with a relatively fast injection is preferred to promote rapid polymerization and reduce the risk of nontarget embolization. For low-flow or distal vessels, a more diluted mixture (eg, up to 1:10) delivered at a slower rate is recommended to allow gradual deposition and enhanced control. In addition, the ratio must be tailored to the distance between the microcatheter tip and the intended embolization site. When embolizing a site far from the catheter, a more diluted mixture can help extend the travel distance and minimize the risk of proximal embolization. Preprocedural Preparation The decision to use NBCA as an embolic agent is guided by the specific clinical indication and the vascular anatomy. Comprehensive preprocedural imaging—including CT, MRI, and diagnostic angiography—is essential for evaluating the vascular anatomy and hemodynamics and identifying potential nontarget structures that are at risk for inadvertent embolization. It cannot be overemphasized that high-quality angiographic assessment is critical to the success of the embolization procedure and for prevention of complications. Special attention should be paid to identifying any concealed or potential collateral pathways between the target vessel and the adjacent vascular territories, as these may serve as conduits for unintended embolic migration. Thorough understanding of such vascular interconnections is vital for safe and effective planning. NBCA-Lipiodol Mixture Preparation The patient and the operator must wear protective goggles. The operator should thoroughly clean any blood from his or her hands and prepare the mixture on a sterile surface. It is recommended that the operator wear an additional pair of sterile gloves during the preparation process. When using transparent NBCA, different syringes should be used for the contrast agent, 5% dextrose, and NBCA-Lipiodol mixture to minimize the risk of confusion. The use of Lipiodol-resistant syringes with distinct colors (eg, Medallion syringe, Merit Medical; NuGen syringe, Shannon) helps to prevent confusion during procedures and enhances operator convenience. In Japan, a purple-colored NBCA formulation that facilitates easier identification is available. The NBCA and Lipiodol should be mixed in a syringe or small sterile bowl immediately before injection to prevent premature polymerization (Movie 2). When mixing is performed by using a pumping technique, a three-way polypropylene stopcock resistant to Lipiodol must be used. Microcatheter Positioning and Simulation The microcatheter is advanced to the target vessel under fluoroscopic guidance, with careful attention paid to ensure that the catheter tip is stable and properly positioned within the intended vessel. Achieving a superselective position minimizes the risk of nontarget embolization and proximal vessel occlusion. If the microcatheter cannot be advanced into the target vessel or a large collateral vessel is present, embolization of the nontarget vessel (or collateral vessel) with use of metallic coils may help to prevent complications associated with nontarget embolization (Fig 4, Movie 3). Test contrast medium injections are performed to confirm the position of the catheter, evaluate the vascular flow pattern, and estimate the volume of the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture required. Because the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture has higher viscosity than contrast medium, the distribution of contrast agent may not be identical to the actual extent of embolization. To mitigate procedural pain caused by the exothermic reaction of NBCA polymerization, 1–2 mL of 1% lidocaine is commonly administered before injecting the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture. Injection of NBCA-Lipiodol Mixture Before the injection of the glue mixture, the microcatheter is flushed with a nonionic solution such as 5% dextrose to prevent premature polymerization within the microcatheter lumen. Use of ionic solutions, including normal saline, must be avoided, as they can induce early NBCA solidification. The NBCA-Lipiodol mixture is injected under continuous fluoroscopic or digital subtraction fluoroscopic guidance until the injection and microcatheter removal are complete. The injection should be performed in a steady controlled manner to ensure that the embolic material fills the intended vessel segment without reflux or inadvertent embolization of nontarget areas. The operator adjusts the injection speed on the basis of several factors: the distance between the microcatheter tip and the target site, the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio, and the flow dynamics of the target vessel. To avoid microcatheter entrapment, the catheter is gently withdrawn following the injection, ideally after sufficient polymerization of NBCA at the target location. In cases in which the microcatheter is firmly wedged into the target vessel and a pressure-dependent injection is performed by using a 1:3 NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio, waiting approximately 3 seconds before removing the catheter is recommended. Fragments of polymerized NBCA may migrate out, causing nontarget embolization, or adhere to the catheter tip and be dislodged during withdrawal, potentially becoming lodged within the angiographic catheter. Approximately 5 mL of blood should be aspirated from the angiographic catheter after microcatheter removal to clear any residual NBCA fragments before postprocedural angiography is performed. Postprocedural angiography is performed to verify the adequacy of embolization and evaluate for any evidence of nontarget embolization or residual perfusion. If necessary, additional embolization with use of metallic coils may be performed.

Applications of Embolization Glue embolization is a versatile interventional procedure used to manage a wide range of vascular and nonvascular conditions. The following section provides a detailed overview of the primary indications for glue embolization, along with the associated technical considerations that are essential for safe and effective practice. Embolization for Arterial Hemorrhage Glue embolization is an effective and versatile method for managing a wide range of arterial hemorrhages, including gastrointestinal (GI) bleeding, hemoptysis, postpartum hemorrhage, musculoskeletal bleeding, and trauma-related vascular injuries. Compared with coil embolization, glue embolization involves a faster procedural time, making it particularly valuable in hemodynamically unstable patients. Moreover, in cases involving small-caliber or highly tortuous vessels, glue can directly reach the bleeding site to achieve hemostasis. Unlike with coils, with which proximal occlusion and thrombosis are required to arrest bleeding, with glue, immediate mechanical occlusion is achieved, rendering it more effective in patients with coagulopathies, who are at higher risk of rebleeding. The efficacy of transarterial embolization for GI bleeding that is unresponsive to endoscopic therapy has been well documented. A variety of embolic agents—including NBCA, coils, gelatin sponge particles, and polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) particles—are used, often in combination, with generally comparable outcomes. The stomach has rich collateral circulation, resulting in a low risk of ischemia after embolization. In contrast, the small intestine and colon have a relatively limited collateral supply and thinner walls, making them more susceptible to ischemic complications following extensive embolization. Animal studies suggest that embolization of one to three vasa recta is relatively safe. Although some interventional radiologists may be hesitant to use glue due to concerns about bowel ischemia, glue is considered safe when it is applied by using a superselective technique. With this approach, a microcatheter with a 1.5F–2.0F tip is wedged into the target vessel, allowing pressure-dependent injection of the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture precisely into the bleeding vasa recta (Fig 5, Movie 4). The authors perform glue embolization in most cases of GI bleeding by using an NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio of 1:3 (25% NBCA). In cases of hemoptysis, gelatin sponge or PVA particles are commonly used. However, compared with bronchial artery embolization (BAE) with PVA, BAE with NBCA offers several advantages, including shorter procedural time, reduced bronchial artery recanalization, and longer hemoptysis-free survival—especially in patients with bronchiectasis. An NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio of 1:2 to 1:4 is typically used, with flow-dependent injection tailored to the size and flow dynamics of the target vessel (Figs 6, 7; Movie 5). For postpartum hemorrhage, a hypervascular uterine blush alone is usually managed by embolizing the bilateral proximal uterine arteries with gelatin sponge particles. However, in rare instances in which active extravasation or a pseudoaneurysm persists after the initial embolization, selective glue embolization may be required. The uterine arteries in postpartum patients are often markedly tortuous, making superselective catheterization challenging, particularly when a pseudoaneurysm is located in the uterine fundus. In such cases, injecting 0.1 mL of the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture into the microcatheter and forcefully flushing it with 5% dextrose enables the glue droplet to traverse the tortuous anatomy effectively, acting like a "bullet" to seal the bleeding point (Fig 8, Movie 6). AVM Embolization Glue embolization offers significant advantages in the treatment of AVMs, whether they are accessed via the transarterial route or direct nidus puncture. The effectiveness of AVM embolization depends largely on the ability of the liquid embolic agent to infiltrate and occlude the nidus, which is the core of the malformation. Historically, NBCA has been the primary embolic agent used for AVM management. However, its role has diminished in recent years with the emergence of nonadhesive liquid embolic agents—ethylene vinyl alcohol (EVOH) copolymer in particular, as it enables more controlled and prolonged injections. EVOH has become the preferred agent for treatment of cerebral AVMs due to its favorable handling characteristics and the associated reduced risk of catheter entrapment. In contrast, for peripheral and visceral AVMs, various embolic agents are used, depending on anatomic and hemodynamic considerations, as well as operator preference. Although absolute ethanol, used alone or in combination with coils, has been used, inexperienced operators may find it particularly demanding to use this agent due to the high risk of complications. In Japan, ethanolamine oleate is frequently used as an alternative to ethanol, but it remains rarely used in other countries. While the use of EVOH as an alternative to NBCA is gradually increasing, advanced techniques such as transvenous or percutaneous nidus puncture with glue embolization continue to be commonly performed. Arterioportal Shunt Embolization In the presence of an arterioportal (AP) shunt, therapeutic agents administered during transarterial chemoembolization or transarterial radioembolization may escape into the portal venous system. This can significantly reduce treatment efficacy and increase the risk of complications such as liver dysfunction. AP shunts commonly arise owing to hepatocellular carcinoma invading the portal vein or as a consequence of prior interventions, including chemoembolization, liver biopsy, and thermal ablation. AP shunts induced by biopsy or ablation often appear as discrete AP fistulas, which are typically amenable to coil embolization. In contrast, AP shunts resulting from previous chemoembolization or portal vein tumor thrombi are generally more complex and often demonstrate vascular patterns reminiscent of AVMs, as they are characterized by multiple feeding arteries and nidus-like shunting zones. Embolization of these complicated AP shunts with use of coils or particulate agents frequently results in incomplete occlusion and high rates of recurrence. Therefore, glue embolization is preferred in these cases. With this technique, the aim is to deliver the embolic agent as close as possible to the shunt point, mirroring the treatment strategy used for AVMs. However, achieving complete resolution of these complicated AP shunts remains a significant technical challenge (Fig 10, Movie 7). Given the high risk of NBCA migration into the portal vein, ethylene vinyl alcohol, which has easier handling properties, may be a more practical option for AP shunt embolization, similar to its use in AVM embolization. Tumor Embolization Glue embolization has a valuable role in tumor embolization, including preoperative devascularization to reduce intraoperative bleeding and management of renal angiomyolipoma and ruptured hepatocellular carcinoma. For optimal results, the embolic agent should be delivered distally, with the microcatheter positioned as close as possible to the tumor. Glue embolization is also emerging as a valuable technique for the treatment of benign prostatic hyperplasia. The procedure involves selective embolization of the prostatic arteries with use of NBCA to reduce perfusion and induce ischemic atrophy of the prostate gland. Early clinical data indicate that NBCA-based embolization is a promising alternative to embolization with conventional agents such as PVA particles or microspheres. During drug-eluting bead transarterial chemoembolization for hepatocellular carcinoma, vascular lakes occasionally may form due to rupture of fragile intratumoral vessels, leading to localized pooling of contrast medium. Although most vascular lakes resolve spontaneously, occasionally they can result in tumor rupture, particularly when they are located near the hepatic capsule. Large gelatin sponge particles are commonly used to occlude the vascular lakes; however, when these are ineffective, NBCA can serve as a reliable alternative for rapid and durable occlusion (Fig 12, Movie 8). Venous Embolization NBCA is increasingly being used in a range of venous embolization procedures, including portal vein embolization (PVE); embolization for gastroesophageal varices, ectopic varices, and lower-limb varicose veins; and gonadal vein embolization. PVE is a well-established preoperative technique aimed at inducing hypertrophy of the future liver remnant in patients scheduled for major hepatic resection. Relatively recent evidence suggests that NBCA-based PVE results in more rapid and significant liver hypertrophy compared with embolization performed by using conventional embolic materials such as PVA particles and coils. This enhanced hypertrophic response may be attributed to a periportal inflammatory reaction induced by NBCA polymerization. In addition, the use of NBCA is associated with reduced contrast medium volume and shorter fluoroscopy times, contributing to procedural efficiency. Despite its benefits, PVE with use of NBCA alone can cause considerable procedural pain due to the exothermic nature of the polymerization. To address this, the authors have adopted the following modified protocol: First, the portal flow is reduced by using PVA particles. The injection of 3–5 mL of lidocaine directly into the portal vein, before NBCA administration, follows. This technique enables the procedure to be performed with use of local anesthesia, improving patient tolerance. Glue embolization is also gaining traction in the management of gastroesophageal and ectopic varices (Fig 14, Movie 9). The capability of NBCA to deeply penetrate variceal and collateral vessels ensures durable occlusion and reduces recurrence. However, use of a meticulous technique is essential to avoid complications such as glue migration into the systemic circulation. To minimize this risk, combination approaches such as concurrent coil and glue embolization and balloon-occluded glue embolization are commonly used. In recent practice, the authors have increasingly favored using a mixture of NBCA, Lipiodol, and ethanol (NLE) for balloon-occluded embolization (Fig 15, Movie 10). NBCA has also emerged as a viable alternative to thermal ablation techniques for the treatment of great saphenous vein (GSV) incompetence in patients with lower-limb varicose veins. In a pivotal study in 2013, a 92% GSV occlusion rate was seen 1 year after NBCA embolization. Subsequent studies have confirmed the high efficacy and safety profile. Advantages of NBCA-based endovenous therapy include shorter procedure duration, absence of tumescent anesthesia, and absence of postprocedural compression therapy. A range of embolic agents, including coils, vascular plugs, detachable balloons, sclerosing agents, and NBCA, have been used to treat male varicocele, a common cause of infertility and scrotal discomfort. Among these, NBCA, as compared with traditional modalities, offers several advantages: reduced radiation exposure, lower rates of postprocedural pain, and decreased recurrence. The ability of NBCA to permeate collateral channels enhances the completeness of embolization and contributes to superior long-term outcomes. Percutaneous Tract Embolization The percutaneous transhepatic or transsplenic approach is frequently used for portal vein interventions, including portal vein embolization and stent placement. Following vascular access, a 5F–10F sheath is inserted into the hepatic or splenic parenchyma. On completion of the procedure, tract embolization is performed to minimize the risk of bleeding. For transhepatic tract embolization, the angiographic catheter is advanced approximately 5 mm beyond the tip of the sheath; then, 33% NBCA is slowly injected through the catheter while the sheath and the catheter are simultaneously withdrawn. In the case of transsplenic tract embolization, to prevent the NBCA from flowing into the portal vein, the distal portion of the tract, immediately adjacent to the splenic vein, is first embolized with one or two metallic coils. Subsequently, the proximal tract is sealed by injecting a glue mixture during gradual sheath withdrawal. Endoleak Embolization Type 2 endoleaks, the most frequently encountered form of endoleaks following endovascular aneurysm repair, result from retrograde blood flow into the excluded aneurysm sac through collateral vessels such as the inferior mesenteric artery and lumbar arteries. The optimal treatment approach (transarterial, percutaneous, or perigraft) remains a subject of ongoing debate. Once access to the aneurysm sac is achieved, embolic agents such as ethylene vinyl alcohol, coils, NBCA, or a combination of these may be used. Ideally, embolization of the entire endoleak cavity, the inflow vessel, and, if present, the outflow vessel (Fig 16, Movie 11) should be performed. However, this can often be technically challenging. Ethylene vinyl alcohol is increasingly being used due to its relative ease of handling. Lymphatic Embolization Thoracic duct embolization is a cornerstone procedure in lymphatic interventions that is typically performed by using a combination of coils and glue. A critical consideration is that NBCA polymerizes significantly slower in lymphatic fluid than in blood. Consequently, use of a high-concentration glue mixture (33%–50% NBCA) is preferred to minimize the risk of unintended glue migration. Coils are often used adjunctively to provide a surface for glue to solidify rather than occlude the thoracic duct by itself. The microcatheter is positioned just proximal to the coil mass as the glue starts to be injected and then slowly withdrawn while the glue is continuously being injected to fully cast the thoracic duct until it comes out of the thoracic duct or cisterna chyli puncture site. Superselective embolization at the leakage point, if present, may occasionally be performed to preserve the main thoracic duct (Fig 17, Movie 12). For lymphatic leaks in the inguinal region, the leakage often originates from extremely thin lymphatic vessels connected to the lymph nodes or crushed lymph nodes themselves. Visualizing the leakage can be facilitated by injecting contrast medium or Lipiodol directly into the upstream lymph nodes. Direct injection of NBCA into the accessed lymph node allows the glue to fill the lymph node parenchyma initially and then progress to the leakage site, effectively sealing the lymphatic leak (Fig 18, Movie 13). The NBCA is diluted with Lipiodol at a ratio of 1:2 or 1:4 (20%–33% NBCA), depending on the distance between the accessed lymph node and the leakage site, as well as the extent of interstitial lymphatics that the glue must traverse before reaching the leakage site. Nonvascular Embolization Glue-based embolization has emerged as a highly effective therapeutic approach for managing complex conditions such as bowel fistula closure, bladder leakage repair, and cutaneous fistula management. In the treatment of bowel fistulas, NBCA is administered with fluoroscopic or CT guidance to occlude the fistulous tract, facilitating tissue healing in the surrounding area. Similarly, bladder leakage caused by trauma, surgical complications, and/or pathologic conditions can be effectively managed with glue embolization, which restores bladder integrity without the need for extensive surgical procedures (Movie 14). With nonvascular embolization, the absence of blood leads to slower glue polymerization and a higher risk of glue migration. As repeat procedures are often necessary, it is advisable to gradually remove the drainage tube along the access route.

Embolization with NLE The NLE mixture forms an embolic agent characterized by significantly reduced adhesiveness compared with the adhesiveness of NBCA alone. The intrinsic high adhesiveness of NBCA can present procedural challenges such as catheter adherence. However, the addition of ethanol to the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture prolongs the polymerization time and substantially reduces adhesiveness. Consequently, NLE can be safely used in balloon-occluded embolization procedures without the risk of balloon catheter adhesion (Figs 15, 16; Movie 10). Although the precise mechanism by which NLE loses its adhesiveness remains unclear, ethanol appears to play a key role, even at low concentrations. Due to the toxicity of ethanol, the authors prefer to use minimal amounts, typically using NBCA-to-Lipiodol-to-ethanol ratios such as 1:3:1 or 2:6:1. When preparing the NLE mixture, Lipiodol and ethanol are mixed first, followed by the addition of NBCA (Movie 2). Balloon-occluded retrograde transvenous obliteration (BRTO) is a widely accepted technique for treatment of gastric varices. However, one of its main limitations is the need to retain the balloon catheter for several hours or even overnight. Plug- and coil-assisted retrograde transvenous obliterations offer an alternative, occluding the gastrorenal shunt with plugs or coils and thus enabling immediate sheath removal. Nevertheless, these techniques may lack the advantage of preprocedural visualization of the gastric varix and afferent vein that balloon occlusion provides. In BRTO performed by using NLE instead of a sclerosing agent, the balloon catheter can be removed instantly. At the authors’ institutions, the gastric varix is filled with a sclerosing agent while the gastrorenal shunt is occluded with the NLE mixture. After a 15-minute dwelling time, the NLE mixture solidifies completely, allowing immediate removal of the balloon catheter and sheath.

Pearls and Pitfalls The advantages of using NBCA versus other embolic materials are particularly evident in situations in which arterial bleeding originates from extremely small (Fig 20, Movie 15) or highly tortuous (Fig 21, Movie 16) vessels that are difficult to access with a microcatheter, or when rapid hemostasis is required due to severe arterial hemorrhage (Fig 22, Movie 17). When the microcatheter is deeply wedged into the vessel, effectively occluding the inflow, filling the catheter and target vessel with 5% dextrose solution before initiating a pressure-dependent glue injection can delay polymerization by minimizing contact with the ionic components. In this scenario, if the microcatheter is withdrawn too early or too abruptly, the glue may migrate distally without facilitating effective occlusion. In such cases, reselecting the target vessel and repeating the embolization procedure may be required (Fig 5, Movie 4). To prevent this, the microcatheter should be gently withdrawn by approximately 5 mm, with a brief 3-second pause following to establish a semiwedged position. If glue is retained within the target vessel, the microcatheter should then be rapidly removed. If antegrade flow resumes and glue begins to migrate distally, a small additional amount of glue may be injected to ensure complete vessel occlusion. A characteristic angiographic feature of a pseudoaneurysm is intermittent visibility of the feeding vessel (Fig 23, Movie 18). During the systolic phase, contrast medium enters the feeding vessel and opacifies the pseudoaneurysm; however, in the diastolic phase, the pseudoaneurysm contracts and nonopacified blood refluxes into the feeding vessel, making it less visible on the digital subtraction angiogram. Caution is warranted when injecting NBCA, as reflux of the embolic agent can also occur during the diastolic phase. If significant reflux is observed during the diastolic phase, it is advisable not to insist on using NBCA. Instead, embolization of the front and back entrances of the pseudoaneurysm with coils may be preferable. Alternatively, partial coil embolization of the feeding vessel followed by advancement of the microcatheter alongside the coil mass into the pseudoaneurysm allows NBCA injection, with the coils acting as a barrier to prevent reflux. Premature polymerization of the glue within the microcatheter can happen, leading to catheter lumen occlusion. This typically results from a too-slow or intermittent injection. If polymerization occurs, it is recommended that the microcatheter be replaced, as attempts to clear the blockage may result in uncontrolled glue expulsion and nontarget embolization. Following glue injection, the microcatheter should be carefully withdrawn to prevent adherence of the catheter tip to the vessel wall. Excessive glue reflux around the microcatheter tip, which increases the risk of catheter adherence, should be minimized by closely monitoring the injection process. In the event of reflux, the catheter tip should be promptly retracted before the completion of polymerization. In cases in which the microcatheter is firmly adhered to the vessel and cannot be retrieved, it is recommended to first cut the microcatheter hub and then remove the sheath and angiographic catheter. Subsequently, the microcatheter shaft should be transected near the skin entry site after gentle traction. The retained microcatheter usually recoils within the vessel and rarely results in clinical complications. When NBCA enters a large nontarget vessel, it typically adheres to the vessel wall and rarely causes significant obstruction to blood flow. In such cases, endovascular retrieval may be attempted by using aspiration through the guiding catheter or a stent-type thrombectomy device (Fig 24, Movie 19). When there is blood flow entering the target site via a collateral vessel, glue injection that is too slow can reduce the main inflow and allow dominant collateral flow to develop, causing the glue to stall before reaching the target (Fig 25, Movie 20). If a collateral vessel is present, it is preferable to embolize it with coils before performing glue embolization. In cases in which coil embolization of the collateral vessel is not feasible, pressure-dependent rapid injection of glue through a wedged microcatheter may help overcome the collateral flow.

Conclusion Glue embolization represents a versatile and effective therapeutic modality for a wide range of clinical indications. The success of this procedure is contingent on comprehensive preprocedural planning, precise microcatheter positioning, and appropriate tailoring of the NBCA-Lipiodol mixture according to the hemodynamic and anatomic characteristics of the target lesion.
