Figure 1. Chemical structure of NBCA. The polymerization of NBCA is initiated by means of contact with the anion. This reaction involves the anionic polymerization mechanism, whereby the cyano group acts as an electron acceptor, initiating the chain reaction.

Figure 2

Drawing illustrates the pressure-dependent injection of glue in a patient with arterial bleeding in the colon.

Full figure caption

Figure 2. (A) Drawing illustrates the pressure-dependent injection of glue in a patient with arterial bleeding in the colon. A microcatheter is advanced until it is wedged within a small bleeding vessel. The glue effectively reaches the bleeding site, with partial spillage into the bowel lumen, leading to hemostasis. (B) Drawing illustrates the flow-dependent injection of glue in a patient with arterial bleeding in the colon. If blood flow is slow and a high concentration of glue (eg, mixed ratio of NBCA to Lipiodol, 1: 2) is used, the glue may not reach the bleeding point and thus reflux around the microcatheter tip. If blood flow is fast and a low concentration of glue (eg, mixed ratio of NBCA to Lipiodol, 1: 6) is used, the glue can reach the bleeding point. Movie 1 shows how blood flow and glue concentration affect the distribution of glue during flow-dependent injection.

Figure 3

Drawing illustrates the flow-dependent injection of glue in a patient with variceal bleeding.

Full figure caption

Figure 3. (A) Drawing illustrates the flow-dependent injection of glue in a patient with variceal bleeding. Distribution of the glue mixture is largely dependent on blood flow speed, injection speed, and the NBCA-to-Lipiodol ratio. (B) Drawing illustrates flow-controlled injection with a microballoon catheter. Movie 1 illustrates the differences in glue distribution patterns achieved with the flow-dependent versus flow-controlled injection method. Movie 2 shows how to mix NBCA and Lipiodol and how to mix NBCA, Lipiodol, and ethanol (NLE).

Figure 24. Cholangiocarcinoma in a 59-year-old man. (A) Portal venogram shows the intact left portal vein after the right anterior and right posterior portal veins were embolized with 355–500-μm PVA particles and the right anterior portal vein (arrowhead) was embolized with glue. Note the drainage tubes in the right and left biliary ducts (arrows). (B) Spot image immediately after embolization of the right posterior portal vein and puncture tract with glue shows washout of glue in the right posterior portal vein (arrows) and migration of glue into the main portal vein (white arrowhead). Note the retained glue in the right anterior portal vein (black arrowhead). The mixing ratio of NBCA to Lipiodol was 1:4. It was presumed that an iatrogenic anteroposterior shunt developed at the sheath insertion site and that arterial inflow into the portal vein following sheath removal contributed to the migration of glue into the main portal vein. (C) Spot image 1 minute after embolization of the right posterior portal vein and puncture tract with glue shows washout of glue in the right posterior portal vein (arrows) and migration of glue into the left portal vein (white arrowheads). Note the retained glue in the right anterior portal vein (black arrowhead). Movie 19 illustrates removal of the glue mixture that migrated into the left portal vein with use of a 7F guiding catheter, stone basket, and clot retrieval stent. (D) Photograph shows the glue mixture removed from the left portal vein. The left portal vein was fully recovered, and the patient underwent right hemihepatectomy 1 month later.
