Cranial Nerve (CN) I   Olfactory Nerve
Function
Origin                 Provide afferent sensory information for smell to the central nervous system
Foramina               Primary afferent neurosensory cells in the roof of both nasal cavities
Destination            Efferent axons travel through numerous perforations within the cribriform plate of the ethmoid bone.
Pathways in brain      Secondary sensory neurons within the olfactory bulbs, which lie in the olfactory groove
                       Olfactory bulb becomes the olfactory tract, which divides into the lateral, intermediate, and medial striae. Largest amount of
Important pathology
                          fibers travel within the lateral striae to the inferior medial temporal lobe amygdala
                       1. Dysfunction results in anosmia with traumatic injury as leading etiology
                       2. Esthesioneuroblatoma: A tumor arising from the olfactory epithelium

TEACHING POINTS CN II SUMMARY

Cranial Nerve (CN) II  Optic Nerve
Function
Origin                 Vision
Foramina
Destination            Photoreceptors in the retina
Pathways in brain
                       Optic canal
Important pathology
                       Optic chiasm

                       Extending posterior to the optic chiasm is the optic tract, which then divides into the lateral branch with fibers traversing to
                          the lateral geniculate body of the thalamus and the medial branch with fibers traversing to the medial geniculate body
                          of the thalamus. Efferent axons lead from the lateral geniculate body form the optic radiation terminating within the
                          calcarine cortex

                       Visual deficits depending on portion of optic nerve affected, including demyelinating, ischemic, infectious/inflammatory,
                          toxic/metabolic, neoplastic, and congenital processes, as well as glaucoma

                        Glossopharyngeal nuclei are in the medulla posterior to the inferior olivary nucleus:

                        4. Sinus nerve: Carotid sinus and carotid body

Function
                        Motor to muscles of the pharynx and larynx
Central nervous system  Sensory from the larynx, trachea, esophagus, and abdominal viscera
   origin/Termination   Taste from the epiglottis and vallecula
                        Sensory to external tympanic membrane and external auditory canal
Intracranial pathway
Foramina                Three parent nuclei within the medulla
Extracranial branches/
                        1. Nucleus ambiguous gives rise to motor fibers to the larynx and pharynx
   Destination          2. Dorsal nucleus receives sensory information and transmits motor information to and from the cardiovascular, pulmo-

Important pathology        nary, and gastrointestinal tracts
                        3. Nucleus of the solitary tract receives taste afferents from the epiglottis and vallecula

                        The vagus nerve exits the medulla through the postolivary sulcus.

                        Pars vascularis of the jugular foramen

                        1. Recurrent laryngeal nerve loops under the aorta on the left side and subclavian artery on the right and travels in the
                           tracheoesophageal grooves and provides motor to all laryngeal muscles except the cricothyroid muscle

                        2. Superior laryngeal nerve: Motor to the cricothyroid muscle
                        3. Arnold nerve: Sensation of the tympanic membrane and external auditory canal

                        Vocal cord paralysis from recurrent laryngeal nerve dysfunction:
                        If causative lesion between the medulla and hyoid bone: CN IX-XII involved with oropharyngeal and laryngeal dysfunction
                        If causative lesion below the hyoid bone: Isolated CN X with only laryngeal dysfunction
