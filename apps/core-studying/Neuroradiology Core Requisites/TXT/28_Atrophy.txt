   presents in older subjects than PD and accounts for 25% of

   usually in memory, attention, executive function, and
   visuospatial and visuoconstructional abilities.

Hyperglycemia

 Hyperglycemia can have a widely variable clinical
   presentation, ranging from increased thirst to seizures,
   stupor, and coma.

 Increased attenuation in the caudate and globus pallidus
   can be seen on CT, and these structures show increased
   signal on T1W1. The signal abnormality is usually bilat-
   eral but can be unilateral as well (Fig. 7.29). The imaging
   findings resolve after hyperglycemia correction, lagging
   behind clinical improvement.

 Finding of cerebral edema can be seen in patients pre-
   senting in diabetic ketoacidosis.

Hypoglycemia

 Patients with profound hypoglycemia can present with
   seizures and coma.

 In severe cases, increased T2 signal and diffusion restric-
   tion can be seen in the cortex, hippocampi, and basal
   ganglia. The thalamus, brainstem, and cerebellum are
   spared.

 When one adds poor nutrition and thiamine deficiency
   to the alcohol abuse, the midbrain, mammillary bodies,
   and basal ganglia are damaged. This condition is known

   is invoked. Although chronic alcoholism is the most
   common cause of Wernicke encephalopathy, other pos-
   sible etiologies include bariatric surgery, gastric bypass,
   hyperemesis gravidarum, prolonged infectious-febrile
   conditions, carcinoma, anorexia nervosa, and pro-
   longed voluntary starvation.
