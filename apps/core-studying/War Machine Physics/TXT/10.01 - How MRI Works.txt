How does MRI work?
A certain percentage of nuclei in the body are considered “ active." WTF does “ active ” mean?
Basically it means that there is NOT an equal number or protons / neutrons. I think of these
atoms as being off balance. Off balance atoms will “ spin” in the magnet or can be “ knocked off
balance.” The classic example is Hydrogen I - of which there is a shit ton in your body (tat,
water, etc..), but you could in theory do MRI targeted at Carbon 13, Oxygen 17, Fluorine 19, and
so on. Having said that - from here on out we are going to be talking about Hydrogen 1 nuclei -
which is basically 1 proton. So when I say “ the proton” - I’m talking about the solitary proton in
a hydrogen nucleus.
The MRI magnet is always “ on” - constantly generating a magnet field. Prior to entering the
magnet, the “ protons” are normally randomly aligned , about half up and half down. When you
stick someone in the magnet slightly more than half of them will align with the direction of the
magnetic field. You can then slap them down with an RF pulse, and then watch how fast they
bounce back up. The micro-environment that these protons live - be it water, fat, the liver etc...
will be slightly different and the complex interactions within these environments will cause the
protons to bounce back from the “ slap down” at different speeds. The difference between how
fast different protons bounce up is the “ contrast” used to tell things apart - and make a pretty
picture.
Think about it like this - you have a fat guy and a skinny guy on a track. One way to tell them
apart is the time it takes them to run a lap. This is the general idea for MRI .
