Different Target
Target Anodes: Mammo uses a molybdenum or rhodium anode (general radiology
uses tungsten).
Why? Remember, characteristic x-rays are “characteristic” because they depend on
the binding energy of the shell involved, and this binding energy is different for
different targets. So its unique... or “characteristic.” Tungsten , which is used in
conventional imaging, has characteristic x-rays around 69.5. Mammo needs energies
way lower than that (ideally between 16 -23). So the solution is to use a low atomic
number target. The classic examples being molybdenum and rhodium. These targets
generate lower K-characteristic x-rays which arc required to generate all those false
positives and unnecessary biopsies that mammo uses to fund its section. I mean... to
find the cancers.
The Normal Spectrum of Molybdenum:
The combination of low kVp and a low Z give
Moly a high peak of characteristic x-rays, with
low Bremsstrahlung. Notice that energy is around
18 (instead of 69.5 like Tungsten).
