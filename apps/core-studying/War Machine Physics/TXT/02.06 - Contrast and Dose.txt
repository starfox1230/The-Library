Contrast and Dose
Back in Chapter 1 - 1 briefly mentioned this thing called the “15% Rule of
Thumb.” Basically it says that X-ray production increases in a linear
direction with mA. Doubling mA will double intensity of the x-ray spectrum.
BUT Increasing kVp bv 15% will double the intensity of the spectrum.
In other words - if you want to increase the x-ray exposure you can either
(1) double your mAs or (2) increase the kVp by 15%.
Why you might do one vs the other depends on the need of the study. Don’t
forget that the average energy of the x-ray spectrum defines the attenuation
characteristics and defines the contrast between objects and tissue.
Want to visualize low contrast objects / tissue ? - Keep the kVp constant and
increase the mAs. You go high on that kVp and you are going to lose all your
RE.
Want to lower dose but maintain a constant exposure ? Raise the kVp by
15%, then lower the mAs by 50%. *This works because the higher kVp x-
rays will penetrate more easily and delivers a lower dose (*skin dose) - see
page 94 to alleviate noggin scratching secondary to confusion.
The study is contrasted ? Remember you are going to want the kVp set at
least twice the binding energy of the contrast agent being used. Iodine - K
edge is 33 keV; so you want it set at least 66 kVp. Barium - K edge is 37 kev,
so you want it set at least 74 kVp
Pediatric (Newborn) Considerations
When you x-ray an infant you are going to do a couple of things differently
(which makes them testable).
(1) You do NOT use a Grid (I’ll talk about grids in Chapter 3).
(2)
You lower the kVp - “good technique is around 65 kVp." They are
small and don’t require a lot of juice to penetrate. *Most adult CXRs
are around 120-140 kVp.
(3)
You use around the same or lower mAs - “good technique is around
2-4 mAs.” Adults are typically around 4 mAs (portables are usually
closer to 2.5).
