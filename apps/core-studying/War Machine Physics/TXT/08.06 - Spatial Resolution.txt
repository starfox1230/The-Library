Axial Resolution: I he ability to tell two closely spaced objects apart in the direction of
the beam , l or optimal axial resolution you need the returning echoes to be separate and not
overlap. The minimum required separation between two reflectors is Vi the spatial pulse
length (SPL), otherwise the returning echoes will overlap.
What is this "Spatial pulse length" ? This is the number of cycles emitted per pulse by the
transducer multiplied by the wavelength.
Objects closer than Vi Spatial Pulse Length will NOT be resolved.

Lateral Resolution: The ability of the
system to resolve objects in a direction
perpendicular to the beam direction. If you
want to tell things apart, they must fit into
separate beams. The thinner the beam, the
more likely that each will fall into a separate
beam. You can reason, that improving lateral
resolution has a lot to do with making your
beam thinner. The beam is most skinny at
the end of the near field (at the focal zone).
Lateral resolution is worst in areas close to and
far from the transducer surface (away from the
focal zone).

Will cranking up the gain help? No - Gain widens the beam. You want a nice narrow beam.

Elevation Resolution (Slice Thickness)- It's the same as
lateral resolution hut is measured in the plane orthogonal to the
image plane. This is usually the worst measure of resolution. It
depends on the transdueer element height. This type of resolution
is why you ean get volume averaging of acoustic details in regions
that are close to the transducer and in the far field.
Things to Know:
•It’s similar lateral resolution but in the orthogonal plane
•It’s typically the weakest link
•It depends on the height of the transducer elements
•It’s a source of volume averaging before and after the focal zone (in the near and far fields)
