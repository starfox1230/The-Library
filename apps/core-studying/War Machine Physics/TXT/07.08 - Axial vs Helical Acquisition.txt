Acquiring the Raw Data
There are basieally two methods Tor doing this: Axial Scanning and Helical Scanning.
Axial
The table is stationary. The tube comes on and takes a picture. The tube shuts oil and the table
moves up a slice. The tube comes back on and takes another picture
Advantages:
•
Better spatial resolution on the /.-dimension since Tull images sets are taken. No partial
volume effect along the long axis.
•
The artifacts of partial volume with helical CT are noticed more along a curved surface.
Classic example = skull.
Helical
The table moves at constant speed. The tube is on the entire time.
Advantages:
•
Primary Advantage = Way faster
•
Secondary Advantage = Post acquisition flexibility in the selection of slice location and
lower probability of anatomic discontinuities between adjacent slices containing moving
anatomy in the chest/abdomen scans
What if you Monkey with the Scanning Beam Width?
• Increased Beam Width = Reduced Scan Time (larger coverage with 1 turn)
• Increased Beam Width = Reduced Motion Artifact (less scan time)
• Increased Beam Width = Increases Partial Volume (more divergent beam)
• Increased Beam Width Does NO T change the radiation dose (mAs in unchanged, even
though the scan time is less, a larger area of tissue is scanned at the same time).
What if you Monkey with the kVp?
• Dose Goes Up: Unlike conventional radiography with automated exposure control,
increasing kVp in CT will increase radiation dose to the patient.
• Increased kVp = Image noise will decrease.
• Iodinated contrast will be more conspicuous at lower kVp (such as 80 kVp), as the
average energy of the x-ray beam will be closer to the k-edge of iodine.
