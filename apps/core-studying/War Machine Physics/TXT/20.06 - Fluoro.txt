Gains
• Flux Gain = Accelerating electrons between voltage differences. (Expressed as a ratio of light
at input to light at output).
• Minification Gain= Electrons from large surface, concentrated on smaller surface
• Brightness Gain = Light increase from the acceleration and concentration processes. Flux
Gain x Minification Gain.
• Conversion Gain: I low good the I.I. is at turning the electrons back into light.
Older I.I.- Increased Dose (the ability of the intensifier to increase brightness deteriorates with
the age of the tube)
Geometric vs Klectronic Magnification
• Geometric Mag = to magnify something, you generally bring it closer to the x-ray source. 11'
you get closer to the tube you get more radiation— and it doesn't double, it squares (inverse
square law in reverse).
• Electronic Mag (Zoom) = Essentially magnification that occurs from projecting a small field of view
onto the matrix of detectors.
There is an increase in dose - driven by the automatic brightness control
(typically 1 ,4x-2.0x per setting). This will increase spatial resolution, and unlike geometric mag will
not lead to focal spot blurring.
• Dose : Geometric Mag > Electronic Mag
• Focal Spot Blur will occur with Geometric Mag
• Electronic Mag increases the Air Kerma (and therefore skin dose). Why? Should be intuitive -
you turned the juice up. More juice concentrated on a smaller spot.
• Electronic Mag does NOT increase the KAP. Why? Because even though you increased the
juice you also decreased the cross sectional area. They cancel out - keeping the KAP the same.
• Best Position of the II. and X-ray Tube? X- ray tube far away, with the 1.1. close.
• Where is the ideal place to stand? On the same side of the patient as the imaging intensifier
It is preferable to position the C arm x-ray tube under the table
• Double the distance from the tube does what to dose? Decreases it by a factor of 4 (inverse
square law).
• Normal Air Kerma Limit = 87 mGy/min (10 Roentgens per min)
• High Level Control (Really Fat Level Control) = 176 mGy/min (20 Roentgens per min)
• In "high level mode ”, you must have audible or visual alarms (in addition to the normal time
alarm used in normal fluoroscopy.)
Pulse Fluoro
• Conventional fluoro = long very low continuous mA. Pulse fluoro is pulsed (NOT continuous
mA but instead pulse of higher mA).
• Pulse Fluoro is good for moving patients (Wiggling Babies) - Gives you sharper images with
less motion blur
• Pulsed Fluoro can reduce dose - when the frame rate is below 30 frame /second
• People always use a drop of 30 to 15 frames per second as an example because that equals a
dose reduction of 30%. Math to get there isn't important, just understand it's not a direct 1:1
thing. 50% reduction in pulse rate
30 % reduction in dose.
• Be careful how the question is worded - because a lower frame rate will have more mA per
individual pulse - but the overall mAs will be decreased relative to regular fluoro below 30
frames per second.
