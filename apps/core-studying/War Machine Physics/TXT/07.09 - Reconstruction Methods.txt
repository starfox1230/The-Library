Projection
Generating an image from the aequired data involves figuring out the linear attenuation
cocfYieicnts of each pixel in the image matrix. In other words... math. Lots of math.
Fortunately computers are good at math. There are a few different techniques for creating the
image.
Buck Projection- This was the original way. After doing lots of math (literally millions of
data points) - using sonograms etc... Eventually a value of attenuation is calculated for each
pixel along a ray - traced through the patient.
Filter Buck Projection- This is the more modern way. Essentially a mathematical filter is
applied prior to calculating the back projection. Just know it’s better than the old way - and
it’s utilized by a lot of modern scanners now.
Perulive Reconstruction - “ Forwarded” information is compared to actual information and
differences are used to correct the image. What you need to know is (a) it can correct for
noise, so you can (b) use a lower dose. It requires some major computer power, that's why
older systems couldn’t use it.

Mathematical Filter (Kernel)
When people say "bone kernel " or "soft tissue kernel’’ - they arc referring to a reconstruction
algorithm (math) also referred to as a “ filter.”
What is actually happening is a tradeoff decision between spatial resolution and noise for each
kernel. The selection is based on clinical parameters.
"Smooth
kernel generates images with low noise but with reduced spatial resolution. This is
what you think about with a soft tissue kernel - like the “ brain kernel.”
"Shurp ' kernel generates images with higher spatial resolution but increased noise. This is what
you think about with a “ bone kernel" - the one you’d use for the skull, or the spine.
