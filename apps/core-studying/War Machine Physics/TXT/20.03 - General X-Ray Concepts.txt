General X-Ray Concepts
Noise
• Data that contributes nothing useful to the image
• “ Quantum Mottle” - major contributor to noise. Essentially a lack of photons hitting the
target.
• “ Scatter” - from compton interactions also contributes to noise
• Field size / Collimation - Collimation will decrease scatter but also decrease overall
photons. Assuming the mAs has been corrected to compensate collimation will improve
Signal to Noise —
but that requires a leap of faith, depending on the writing of the
question.
• Grids - decrease scatter, and increase dose (via - requiring an increase in mAs)
• “ Grid Cut Off’- Creation of quantum mottle by blocking to many x-rays, usually from
putting the grid on wrong (slap the tech around).
• Noise increases as the source gets further away - inverse square law
Spatial Resolution
• Limiting factors for spatial resolution;
• Film = Size of the Grain of Photographic Chemical
• Computed Radiography (CR) = Size of the Laser used to read the phosphor plate in the
cassette reader
• Digital Radiography (DR) = Size of the individual thermoluminescent transistor.
•
Focal Spot: Small Focal Spot = Less Blur
•
Source-Object Distance: Closer the source is to the image = More Blur
•
Object Detector Distance: Closer the object is to the detector = Less Blur
•
Magnification: More Magnification = More Blur

• Magnification
• Increases with the patient far away from the detector. “ Greater Object to Detector
Distance.”
• Increases with the source closer to the patient. “ Less Source to Object Distance”
• Modulation Transfer Function (MTF) - The ability of a system to maintain the signal
eontrast as a function of spatial resolution. The way I remember this is that it’s harder and
harder to keep up the signal when you want to look at smaller and smaller line pairs, see
page 48 if confusion persists
• Detective Quantum Efficiency (DQE) - DQE is essentially an estimate of the required
exposure level that will be necessary to create an optimal image. High DQE = Low Dose,
Low DQE = I ligh Dose, see page 48 if confusion persists
Contrast Resolution
• kVp is the power player —
low kVp = high contrast
• Reducing scatter (collimation and grids) improve contrast
• k-edge, landing on or near it - greatly improves contrast
Brightness
• Digital has this larger dynamic range (compared to plain film) so you can correct
moderately under or over exposed images by adjusting the brightness (via the window
level / center)
