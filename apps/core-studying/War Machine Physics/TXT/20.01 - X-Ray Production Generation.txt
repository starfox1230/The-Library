X-Ray Production / Generation
• Ionization - If the energy of the incident particle is enough to eject an electron then you have
ionization. If general a photon with energy greater than 15ev would be ionizing.
• General X-Ray Targets arc Made of Tungsten - Because of it’s high Z. X-ray yield is
proportional to Z squared.
• Electrons from a heated filament will be pushed / pulled towards a target. When they strike
the target 3 basic things happen: ( 1 ) Heat Production, (2) Characteristic Interactions, and (3)
Brems Interactions
• “ Characteristic Interactions” = Occur when an inner electron is forced out, and it’s spot is
filled by an outer electron. The exchange results in release of potential energy in the form of a
“ characteristic photon.” This makes the spiky thing on the spectrum diagrams.
• The energy of the characteristic photon is specific to the shell and the target. The classic 69.5
K. shell Tungsten.
• Brems interactions occur from “ Breaking” or “ Slowing Down” of the electrons around a target
nucleus. It occurs in a spectrum - with different energy photons given off depending on high
close the electron is to the nucleus (how much slowing down it does). This makes the fat
mountain looking part on the spectrum diagrams.
• The average Brems energy is equal to one third of the kVp selected. The peak energy is the
kVp. In a standard Tungsten target x-ray tube with normal filtration, the average energy is
going to be between 1/3 to 1/2 the maximum energy. - Example kVp 70 = Average of 25
• Quantity (total number of x-rays) is directly proportional to mAs. Double = Double. Quantity
varies as the approximate square of the change in kVp. Double = Increased Quantity by Four.
Quantity decreases with distance - via the inverse square law (change as the inverse of the
square of the distance).
• A 15% kVp increase is the same as doubling the mA
• Quality (penetrating power) is affected by (1 ) kVp -directly, and (2) Filtration. Quality is
measuring in “ half value thickness” hence penetrating power.

• ( 1 ) mA = Beam Quantity
• (2) kVp = Beam Quality (and quantity)
• (3) Filtration = Beam Quality (and quantity)
• (4) Target Material = Changes Charactcristie Peaks. Also increasing the atomic number of
the target will increase x-ray quantity * one of the reasons tungsten is used. Also the quality
is changed or “ shifted to the right”
• (5) Generator Type = Improving the efficiency of the generator increases the x-ray beam
quantity and quality.
• 1lighcr Z = More Bremsstrahlung
• In nuclear medicine, low Z materials (plastic) are used to shield Beta emitters (classical
example = 90 Yttrium) in order to minimize bremsstrahlung production. Lead would make
this much worse.
• Filtration: If the beam is filtered the wimpy low energy photons will be removed first
(leaving the higher ones). This will increase the average photon energy. Since average
photon energy determines penetration capacity this will increase the HVL.
• Average energy increases with more filtration
