A 2-year-old male patient presents with facial weakness and worsening somnolence, nausea, and vomiting. Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?

Your answerB
Correct answerB. Brainstem glioma
Vital Concept

Diffuse intrinsic pontine/brainstem glioma appears as an expansile infiltrative tumor centered in the pons, extending to middle cerebellar peduncles, midbrain, and medulla. It shows hyperintensity on T2-weighted and FLAIR images with minimal or no enhancement. The tumor frequently engulfs the basilar artery and shows exophytic growth into the prepontine cistern.

Explanation

Brainstem glioma

Brainstem gliomas are tumors of the pediatric brain and can be characterized as two major types. One type is the diffuse midline glioma, and the other type is focal glioma involving varying portions of the brainstem. The diffuse midline glioma is a WHO grade IV expansile, infiltrating lesion with characteristic low attenuation on CT and hyperintense T2 signal (pink arrow) without significant enhancement. They typically produce an expansile appearance of the pons (blue arrow), which engulfs adjacent structures such as the basilar artery (purple arrow).

Diffuse midline gliomas carry a poor prognosis and are typically not amenable to surgical resection, while they are poorly responsive to chemotherapy and radiation. They are more commonly seen in the setting of neurofibromatosis type 1. Focal types of brainstem gliomas, such as tectal gliomas, are typically WHO grade I/II lesions, which commonly respond to radiation and carry a better prognosis, very rarely requiring surgical resection.

Incorrect Answers:

A. Medulloblastoma is a WHO grade IV tumor arising from the superior medullary velum in the roof of the fourth ventricle (in children) and from the cerebellar hemisphere (more lateral, more common in adults). They tend to be spherical in shape and are hyperdense but heterogeneously calcified and hemorrhagic on CT, typically enhancing after administration of contrast.

On MRI they are often markedly heterogeneous on T1- and T2-weighted imaging with heterogeneous enhancement and characteristic restricted diffusion. They characteristically spread by subarachnoid seeding and as such, the entire neuraxis should be imaged prior to surgical resection. They typically present with posterior fossa signs such as ataxia and cranial neuropathies.

C. Glioblastoma multiforme (GBM) is a WHO grade IV malignant tumor composed of astrocytes and is the most common intracranial neoplasm in adult patients. It is characterized by a thick enhancing periphery of the tumor mass with internal necrosis. Surrounding edema represents infiltrative tumor extension. Frequently, they cross the midline via the corpus callosum (“butterfly glioma”). In children, GBM more frequently involves posterior fossa structures and the brainstem.

D. Osmotic demyelination can mimic a diffusely infiltrating pontine glioma with expansile appearance, edema, infiltration of pontine structures, and restricted diffusion. However, the clinical setting of rapid correction of hyponatremia will often also be given. A reasonable differential diagnosis for the imaging appearance shown it should be accompanied by the appropriate clinical scenario.

E. Pilocytic astrocytomas are WHO grade I tumors that typically present as cystic cerebellar lesions characterized as having a “cyst with enhancing mural nodule” appearance. They may also arise in the optic pathways of patients with neurofibromatosis type 1.

On CT, they have a characteristic low-attenuation cystic appearance with peripheral enhancing nodularity. They may also have peripheral calcifications. On MRI, they are typically markedly T2 hyperintense with an enhancing nodular component along their periphery when encountered in the cerebellum. They are most commonly present in children aged 5 to 15 years and grow slowly, producing symptoms as a result of mass effect, and have a good prognosis when completely resected.

  2. BAngiomyolipoma(92%)

Vital Concept

India ink artifact at the renal mass-kidney interface and loss of lesion signal on out of phase imaging both indicate fat within the lesion, which is diagnostic of angiomyolipoma.

Explanation

Angiomyolipoma

The MRI image shown is an out-of-phase image (T2*GRE). In-phase and out-of-phase images are generally T1-weighted gradient-recall echo (GRE) sequences. What is unique about them is the timing. Because of slightly different microenvironments of fat and water, the protons have slightly different Larmor frequencies. The difference in precession speed means that there will be times when the protons are "in phase" and times when they are "out of phase." At 1.5 T, the signals will be in phase approximately every 4.4 ms and, therefore, out of phase at 2.2 ms, 6.6 ms, and so on. By adjusting the time-to-echo to correlate with the in-phase and out-of-phase times, the in-phase and out-of-phase images are obtained. The main goal is the demonstration of Type 2 chemical shift artifact to show the presence of fat. The dark line surrounding the lesion (and the abdominal organs) is known as "India ink" artifact and is the result of the presence of fat and water protons in the same voxel. Remember, only voxels containing both water and fat will show signal loss on out-of-phase imaging, as opposed to fat-saturation sequences. Pure fat and pure water will not lose signal. You can appreciate the power of the sequence in the shown image. A fat-rich lesion in the right kidney shows a surrounding "India ink artifact" confirming fat in the lesion, which shares voxels with the water-rich kidney along the margins. This is most compatible with angiomyolipoma.

A. A simple renal cyst is hypointense on T1 imaging and hyperintense of T2 imaging.

C. Renal cell carcinoma is heterogeneous on T1 imaging, while T2 imaging depends on the histological type of RCC [clear cell (hyperintense), papillary (hypointense)].

D. Lymphoma would appear hypointense to the surrounding renal parenchyma on T1 imaging and hyperintense to the surrounding renal parenchyma on T2.

E. Pulsation artifact occurs as a result of fluid or tissue moving during the process of the scan, resulting in a phenomenon known as "ghosting" which occurs in the direction of the short axis of the image.
