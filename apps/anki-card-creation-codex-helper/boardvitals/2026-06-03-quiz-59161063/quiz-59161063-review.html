Explanation

Beta minus

The study shown is a post-therapy I-131 scan for the treatment of metastatic thyroid cancer. One can see intense uptake in the thyroid bed causing star artifacts (blue arrows) as well as multiple sites of metastatic disease (red arrows). I-131 is a beta emitter. In this process, a neutron is converted into a proton, an electron, and a subatomic particle called an antineutrino. The electron (or beta particle) is ejected from the atomic nucleus and results in tissue ionization through electrostatic interactions with orbital electrons. Beta particles give up their energy through a series of such interactions along a tortuous path which in soft tissue is relatively close to their source. For this reason, I-131 is used as therapy for thyroid cancer and hyperthyroidism.

Incorrect Answers:

A. Isomeric transition occurs in some decay schemes where there is an intermediate species with measurable half-lives that exist in a metastable state. For these species, an "m" is placed after the mass number to indicate the phenomenon. They decay by gamma emission. As such, the transition from the metastable state to the ground state is isomeric because the atomic number does not change and thus the transformation is from one isomer to another. The most important example of a metastable state in nuclear medicine is technetium-99m.

C. In positron decay, a positive electron is ejected from the nucleus, resulting in a decrease in the atomic number between the parent and the daughter nuclei and an increase in the N/P ratio. Positron decay occurs in nuclides that are neutron poor. The most widely used positron emitter decay is fluorine-18. The minimum transition energy required for positron decay is 1.02 MeV, which is the energy equivalent of the mass of two electrons.

D. Alpha particles are essentially helium nuclei (two protons and two neutrons) with a 2+ charge and an atomic mass number of 4. Alpha decay is common in the higher atomic number range of the periodic table of elements. Alpha particles are used for therapeutic rather than diagnostic purposes because they are large, heavy, and their range in soft tissue is short.
